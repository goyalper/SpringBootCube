The Rubik's Cube

The Rubik's Cube is a 3D combination puzzle invented in 1974 by Hungarian sculptor and professor of architecture Ernő Rubik. Originally called the "Magic Cube", it was licensed by Rubik to be sold by Ideal Toy Corp in 1980 and won the German Game of the Year special award for Best Puzzle that year.

A standard Rubik's Cube has six faces, each covered by nine stickers of six solid colors: white, yellow, orange, red, green, and blue. The puzzle consists of twenty-six miniature cubes, or "cubies", with colored stickers that rotate on a central pivot mechanism.

The puzzle is solved when each face of the cube displays only one color.

Fascinating Facts

  • There are 43,252,003,274,489,856,000 possible configurations of the Rubik's Cube.
  • The current world record for solving the Rubik's Cube is 3.47 seconds, set by Yusheng Du in 2018.
  • Any Rubik's Cube can be solved in 20 moves or less (known as "God's Number").
  • Over 450 million Rubik's Cubes have been sold worldwide, making it the world's top-selling puzzle game.
  • The first world championship was held in Budapest in 1982.

Applications

  • Solving the Rubik's Cube has been used to teach algorithmic thinking and problem-solving skills.
  • The cube has applications in group theory, a branch of abstract algebra.
  • Cube-solving algorithms are used to demonstrate concepts in computer science and artificial intelligence.
  • The puzzle is used in education to illustrate concepts of symmetry, combinatorics, and permutations.
  • Solving techniques have been applied to other sequential decision-making problems.

Cube Notation

Standard Rubik's Cube notation uses letters to represent the face that should be turned, and modifiers to indicate the direction of the turn.

Face Letters

F - Front face

B - Back face

U - Up face

D - Down face

L - Left face

R - Right face

Modifiers

(none) - Clockwise 90° turn

' - Counterclockwise 90° turn

2 - 180° turn (twice)

Examples

R - Right face clockwise

U' - Up face counterclockwise

F2 - Front face 180° turn

L' D R - Sequence of 3 moves

Solving Methods

Beginner's Method

The beginner's method is a layer-by-layer approach that's easy to learn but requires more moves.

  1. Solve the first layer cross
  2. Solve the first layer corners
  3. Solve the middle layer edges
  4. Solve the last layer cross
  5. Position the last layer edges
  6. Position the last layer corners
  7. Orient the last layer corners

CFOP Method (Fridrich Method)

CFOP is the most popular speedcubing method, used by most world champions.

  1. Cross - Solve the bottom cross
  2. F2L - First 2 Layers - Solve corners and edges simultaneously
  3. OLL - Orient Last Layer - Make the top face a single color
  4. PLL - Permute Last Layer - Rearrange the pieces while keeping orientation

Roux Method

An alternative speedcubing method that uses fewer moves and rotations.

  1. Build a 1x2x3 block on the left
  2. Build a 1x2x3 block on the right
  3. Solve the top corners and orient edges
  4. Solve the remaining edges

Kociemba's Algorithm (Two-Phase Algorithm)

This is the algorithm our solver uses. It can find a solution in 20 moves or less.

Developed by Herbert Kociemba, this algorithm works in two phases:

  1. Transform the cube to a state where each face has either the same color or the opposite color in each position
  2. Solve the cube using only half-turn moves of the up and down faces, and quarter and half-turn moves of the other faces

The algorithm is very efficient and can solve any valid cube state optimally or near-optimally.

Tips for Speedcubing

Practice Tips

  • Learn finger tricks to make turns faster and smoother
  • Practice algorithms until they become muscle memory
  • Time your solves and track your progress
  • Break down your solve into steps and practice each one separately
  • Learn to look ahead - plan your next moves while executing the current ones
  • Start slow and focus on accuracy before speed

Equipment Tips

  • Use a good quality speedcube (not the original Rubik's brand)
  • Keep your cube clean and lubricated
  • Adjust the tension to match your turning style
  • Consider the size of the cube - standard is 56mm, but smaller ones may be faster
  • Break in a new cube by doing hundreds of solves
  • Find the right cube for you - different brands have different feels
